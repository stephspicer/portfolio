One app, three brands: theming tokens before Figma variables

We built a theme-able design system foundation and the core user experience for a client managing multiple food delivery brands, navigating the limits of 2021 Figma through close collaboration and a token-based approach and working with developers to ensure scalability, knowing a 4th unknown brand may be coming.

B2C Product Design Client work

Overview

My Role

Product Designer allocated 50%

Timeline

6 months engagement, 2021-2022

Key Tools

Figma

Atlas Theme Switcher & Design Tokens plugins with JSON exports

Context & Challenge

A single platform was needed to unite three distinct food delivery brands

After a three company merger, our clients needed to build a new platform to address the needs of all of their users. They had three different brands, brand loyalty, and wanted to retain each brand and improve their UX, without the technical burden of maintaining separate platforms for each. They partnered with Crema to design and architect this new foundation. The goal was one clean and competitively featured food delivery app UX for all three brands that could visually adapt and even prepare for a fourth unknown brand in the future.

I partnered closely with another Product Designer on this project. We were each allocated at 50% of our time. Our broader team included a Product Manager, Test Engineer, and the development team who would bring our designs to life.

Discovery & Approach

Plugin experimentation led to a token-based theming solution for the UI with JSON exports

In 2021, native Figma features for theme management like variables and modes did not exist. We needed a robust way to manage distinct visual styles and deliver intuitive core flows, all within a single Figma library ensure developers could replicate this efficiently.

The foundation and alignment with eng would be key to meeting all our goals for the 6-month deadline, so we spent several weeks focusing on finding the best approach in collab with our front-end peers. After research and experimentation, we settled on using the Atlas Theme Switcher plugin to manage the visual brand swaps directly within our Figma library ‚Äì colors, type styles, and logos.

We architected the entire system and design file around design tokens, meticulously defining base values for each brand and also for everything else, from spacing units to shadows. But Atlas didn't come with development integration, so after I explored a number of options, we used the Design Tokens plugin to export our tokens as a JSON file. Our dev team were able to implement a parallel theme-switching mechanism in the codebase for the components powering our designed user flows, mirroring our Figma setup.

Design Approach

We designed the core food delivery experience while co-creating the design system

My design partner and I worked in lockstep, tackling both the user experience and the system architecture. We designed all key screens and flows necessary for a food delivery service, including restaurant discovery and search, menu browsing, order customization, account creation and management, checkout, and order tracking. We focused on creating an intuitive and clear path for users through each stage, prototyping and refining these core interactions using our placeholder "Fetch" brand as the neutral base.

This foundational UX work happened in parallel with building the design system, but once the design system groundwork was done, our UI work was much easier. Our collaboration was fluid, allowing us to critique flows, brainstorm UI solutions, and build components together. By the project's end, our individual contributions had totally blended, resulting in a stronger, unified product experience we were both proud of and couldn't even recall who had designed which pieces.

Outcome

We delivered a scalable multi-brand platform foundation and core user experience

The collaboration on this project made us really successful. We were able to meet what felt like an ambitious deadline, even a bit ahead of schedule. And after all our work breaking down themeing, and later documenting every state for each component, the final product looked exactly like our designs. I had a lot of fun exploring this space. Our eventual solution would have been so much easier with today's Figma features, but it was exciting to work on something that requires creative problem solving.
